Based on the graphs, both PM10 and PM2.5 levels show fluctuations over time. The various peaks indicates a significant increase in air pollution on that specific day. The peaks in the graphs could be due to specific factors like increased vehicle emissions, industrial activity, or environmental factors such as weather conditions or wildfires.

Both pollutants exhibit a similar pattern, with peaks occurring around the same time. This suggests that the sources contributing to these pollutants might be related. The PM10 readings are higher in magnitude compared to PM2.5, which is expected since PM10 includes larger particles.

The Air Quality Index (AQI) is a measure created by government agencies to inform the public about current or expected air pollution levels. Higher pollution levels result in a higher AQI.

Similarly, the National Air Quality Index (NAQI) is a measure used to assess and communicate the quality of air based on various pollutants, including PM₂.₅ and PM₁₀. NAQI provides a standardized way to inform the public about air pollution levels and potential health effects.This informaiton is obtained from the research paper titled "An exploratory analysis of PM2.5/PM10 ratio during spring 2016–2018 in Metropolitan Lima", with lead author Rita Jaqueline Cabello‑Torres.

The NAQI for both PM10 and PM2.5 are calculated by us with the equation mentioned in the research paper. The result from our calculation shows a similar pattern to the AQI graph extracted from the public channel in ThingSpeak.




PM2.5 and PM10 Correlation Readings and Data Visualizations




In the PM2.5 and PM10 Correlation Readings and Data Visualizations section, it shows the correlation readings over time for at least 2 weeks.

What is PM Correlation?


PM correlation refers to the statistical relationship between different types of particulate matter (PM) concentrations measured at different locations or between different PM size fractions (e.g., PM2.5 and PM10). Correlation helps understand how PM levels change together over time and space, which is useful for air pollution monitoring, health studies, and environmental policy-making.

Why is PM Correlation Important?


  • - Helps predict air quality trends in unmonitored areas.
  • - Assists in identifying pollution sources and patterns.
  • - Supports health studies linking PM exposure to diseases.
  • - Improves environmental policies for air quality control.


The PM10, PM2.5 and PM2.5-10 concentration graph shows the relationship between PM2.5, PM10, and PM2.5–10 concentrations in terms of their correlation.

Data Points & Trends Description
Black circles (PM10 data points) Show a strong linear relationship with PM2.5, indicating a high correlation between the two.
PM10 Fit (Dashed line) Represents the best-fit line for PM10 data, showing a nearly 1:1 relationship.
White diamonds (PM2.5–10 data points) These data points are much lower and relatively flat compared to PM10.
PM2.5–10 Fit (Dotted line) Shows little variation, meaning PM2.5–10 has a weaker correlation with PM2.5.


Interpretation


High correlation between PM10 and PM2.5:


  • - PM10 levels increase proportionally with PM2.5, meaning PM2.5 is a major contributor to PM10.
  • - This supports the idea that fine particles (PM2.5) make up a significant portion of total particulate matter.

Weak correlation between PM2.5–10 and PM2.5:


  • - The flat trend of PM2.5–10 indicates that it does not vary significantly with PM2.5 levels.
  • - This suggests PM2.5 and PM2.5–10 originate from different sources.
